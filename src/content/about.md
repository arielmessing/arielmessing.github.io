For the past twenty years, I have been exploring the craft of software development. 

What started with an early interest in Mathematics and Computer Science has become a long-term focus on how complex systems are built and maintained: a balance between the discipline of architectural design and the broader, occasionally more chaotic, landscape of engineering — the whole, and the artisan's eye for the individual line of code.

A few years ago, a move to the UK with my wife coincided with the start of the pandemic. That period of forced stoicism in a quiet Kentish village served as an invitation to return to hands-on coding. From machine learning to generative art via the patient nature of open-source contribution, it was a chance to explore corners of computing I had previously left untouched.
  
Before transitioning to freelance work, I was enjoying the mix of high-level vision with practical reality as a developer, systems architect and product manager for a cyber security startup through two successive acquisitions. A professional highlight of those years was a core data flow redesign, born of the pressure of scale, as a serverless solution — later leading the team transforming a fragile prototype into a resilient, production environment.
 
When not at my desk, I might be unravelling some threads of medieval history, tending to a sourdough babka, or cycling through the English countryside.

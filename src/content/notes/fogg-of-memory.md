A quick follow-up on an ironic typo from a paperback classic, mentioned in the closing lines of a [recent note](/notes/phil-vs-the-pathogens).

> **Fogged over**  
>
> SIR--Your "Around the World in Eighty Days" headline on primary school tables (Feb. 23) names the intrepid traveller as "Phileas" Fogg--the spelling now almost universally recognised. In Jules Verne's original book, he was Phineas. How did this change come about? Was Michael "Phileas Fogg" Palin responsible, or perhaps a certain manufacturer of pickles and [snacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phileas_Fogg_snacks)?
>
> <small>**P.J. BATEMAN**</small>  
> *Newton Ferrers, Devon*  

--[*The Daily Telegraph*](https://www.newspapers.com/article/the-daily-telegraph-mandela-effect-phi/108044412/), 2 Mar 1999

Alas, poor P.J. Bateman is mistaken. 

In the definitive 1873 [French edition](https://fr.wikisource.org/wiki/Le_Tour_du_monde_en_quatre-vingts_jours/Texte_entier) (*Le Tour du monde en quatre-vingts jours*), the protagonist's name is most definitely **Phileas**. The "Phineas" variation was actually a common typo in early English translations. 

The confusion Verne sowed 150 years ago is still causing polite debates in the Devon countryside, it seems. But what a great case of the [Mandela effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_memory#Mandela_effect)!

Oh, and Derwent Valley Foods [only produced snacks](https://www.gracesguide.co.uk/Derwent_Valley_Foods).

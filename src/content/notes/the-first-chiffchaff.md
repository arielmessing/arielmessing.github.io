title: "The First Chiffchaff"

date: 2026-04-04
---

Saw my first Chiffchaff today.

We were walking along the Medway towpath, somewhere upstream from Allington Lock, when we heard it. That famously onomatopoeic song, ringing out **loud** from the brambles:

*chiff-chaff-chiff-chaff*


I turned left and there was he,[^1] perched on a branch: olive-brown, conspicuously small, and flicking a tail. Just as the RSPB [describes](https://www.rspb.org.uk/birds-and-wildlife/chiffchaff).

Noticing us, the little singer flitted through the shrubs and disappeared.

Cold spells over Passover/Easter weekend might make spring feel a world away, but this brief encounter[^2] was more than enough to put a smile on our faces.

## Postscript

Amidst all Chiffchaff-related excitement, I forgot to mention our other spotting -- though this one wasn't trying quite so hard to blend in. 

Up in Barming Woods, perched within the still-sparse foliage of a low tree, we saw another bird that is widespread yet (to borrow a term from, I believe, Simon Barnes' delightful *A Bad Birdwatcher’s Companion*) "exotic" in its own way: **the Jay**. 

That brilliant Malachite-blue wing patch!

[^1]: "*She*"? Female Chiffchaffs look the same, but the singing points to the male persuasion.

[^2]: While some now [over-winter in the UK](https://www.bto.org/learn/about-birds/birdfacts/chiffchaff), the species remains one of the earliest migratory warblers to return en masse from Africa and the Mediterranean each spring.

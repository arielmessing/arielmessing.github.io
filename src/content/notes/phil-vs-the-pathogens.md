---
title: "Phil vs. the Pathogens"
description: "Picked up an old paperback and fell down a rabbit hole of Victorian spelling, colonial Aden, and the medical history hidden between the lines of Jules Verne."
date: 2026-04-09
---

Over the course of this long Easter weekend I've been traversing the highly enjoyable classic, *Around the World in Eighty Days*.

Travelling by sea from Suez to Bombay, the noble *Mongolia* docked at Steamer Point, Aden, to coal up. Meanwhile, our protagonists -- still early in their journey -- went ashore to have their visas stamped at what Verne calls 'the Gibraltar of the Indian Ocean'.

> The visa procured, Mr. Fogg returned on board to resume his former habits; while Passepartout, according to custom, sauntered about among the mixed population of Somanlis, Banyans, Parsees, Jews, Arabs, and Europeans who comprise the twenty-five thousand inhabitants of Aden.

In this multicultural melting pot, the term **Somanlis** caught my eye. I naturally assumed it was a typo, perhaps a quirk of the paperback edition[^1] I'm holding, picked up from a street library.

Somanlis, it appears, was simply a Victorian-era spelling for Somalis -- a common variation in English and French at a time when standardised spelling for non-European ethnic groups was still evolving:

> ADEN, November 22. News has been received here that a tribe of Somanlis at Core Ambadu, Gulf of Tadjurra, had made a sudden attack on the crew of the French warship Pengouin while engaged in fetching fresh water on board. […]

--<small>MASSACRE OF FRENCH SEAMEN BY SOUDANESE</small>,[^2] [*The South Australian Advertiser*, 24 Nov 1886](https://trove.nla.gov.au/newspaper/article/37163035).

Nevertheless, searching for this particular quote brought me across some illuminating -- almost interdisciplinary -- reading. In an article titled 'Epidemiological risks in international travel in the 19th century',[^3] Jules Verne's fiction is used as a case study for historical medical challenges. What an irony: while racing the sun, Fogg et al. had unwittingly outrun several actual pandemics. And we haven't even sighted Bombay yet. 

Take, for example, the case of the [Cisterns of Tawila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cisterns_of_Tawila) 'where the English engineers were still at work,' according to Verne, 'two thousand years after the engineers of Solomon.' While impressive, the study's authors note the vast artificial rainwater reservoirs were 'perfect breeding grounds' for [cholera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cholera). 

Not that Fogg needed any closer brush with the bacterium. The book's opening lines declare that --

> Mr. Phileas Fogg lived, in 1872, at No. 7, Saville [sic] Row, Burlington Gardens, the house in which Sheridan died in 1814 […] It was at least certain that Phileas Fogg had not absented himself from London for many years.

-- which allows the study to trace a 'possible' route from Fogg's residence to the Reform Club: a path that skirts the focal point of the 1854 cholera outbreak.[^4]

Or the [smallpox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smallpox) pandemic of 1870-4. Spread in the wake of the [Franco-Prussian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War) and sweeping through most of Europe, 'the worst smallpox outbreak in the nineteenth century' caused the death of more than 40,000 people in England (and an estimated half a million across the continent). Tighter legislation through [the Vaccination Act of 1867](https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/Vict/30-31/84/enacted) ensured that by the middle of this period most Londoners were vaccinated -- including, we can assume, our hero. Yet it is curious that the word 'smallpox' is not mentioned once in the novel. 

The very stoicism and isolation that made the man seem so cold were perhaps his best defense against a world teeming with invisible dangers. One can't help but look with admiration. He moved through those great plagues of his century with the same unflappable indifference he showed toward the famous elephant ride to Allahabad, proving that nothing -- not even a global pandemic -- could break the stride of Phileas Fogg.[^5]

[^1]: Minster Classics, 1968 (Printed in the U.S.A); Complete and Unabridged. U.K. 95p; Australia $2.95; New Zealand $2.85; South Africa R1.80; Canada $2.75; Malta 1.00c. 

[^2]: Note, of course, **Soudanese**. Lumping distinct East African groups together under broad, shifting labels seems to be another Victorian habit -- echoed down under.

[^3]: Published in the *annus horribilis* of 2021.

[^4]: Centred around a certain contaminated water source in Soho, this was 'the first epidemic in the history of medicine to be the subject of a full-scale epidemiological study'. The removal of the water pump's handle by [Dr. John Snow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Snow) stopped new cases from appearing.

[^5]: This edition spells his name once as 'Phineas'. Now *that's* a typo.

Six o'clock in the morning, and I am cycling down the path around the quarry[^1] in the middle of *"our"* woods.

As I listen to the [melody of chiffchaffs](/notes/the-first-chiffchaff) and the machine-gun racket of wrens[^2] (rowdy little buggers), the moderate incline slows me down. Now the trees to my left clear to reveal a grassy hillside, some shrubs, a gravel path forking towards a fence and beyond it, a vision of fields further away. A bunny rabbit crosses the road ahead of me onto the grass, and I momentarily ponder the effectiveness of a bright white bum on a small, prey animal[^3].

Then I see them: a pair of reddish-brown **Roe deer** (or so they seem to my untrained eye, which didn't spot any spots).

They are standing on both sides of the path to the left, looking startled. And quite young. They don't give me enough time to wish I had brought my camera with me, or at least the phone: the one on the left -- "behind" me, as it were, bounces into the bushes and disappears. The other seems more reluctant. Moves towards the path, in an attempt to follow; feels exposed -- practically in my way -- and hops back. Then turns around... and repeats the movements, before coming to a decision and runs into the undergrowth.

I have been cycling in these parts for -- how long? Five years, at least. Even this early in the morning. And never, ever, even thought there are deer in the woods. Goes to show the folly in claiming you really *know* a place -- even one right at your doorstep.

## Postscript

The blue-grey limestone quarried around Maidstone since Roman times is famously known as **Kentish Ragstone**. Incredibly tough and stubborn, this rubble-like rock was used for heavy-duty engineering and prestigious projects alike. 

Carried in barges down the River Medway to London, it was employed in the monumental construction of the Tower of London and Westminster Abbey, to name but a few. So were Kent's own medieval jewels like the Archbishop's Palace in Maidstone, Dover Castle, and Knole House on the outskirts of Sevenoaks: an estate famous, fittingly enough, for its very own wild herds of deer.

[^1]: Would have loved to say *"ye olde quarry"*, for dramatic effect, as the local stone has been in use for centuries constructing some of England's finest piles. But it is definitely an active one: the occasional siren, an echo of a remote blast -- though gratefully not this early.

[^2]: Ornithologists note that per unit of weight, a wren sings with [ten times](https://www.allaboutbirds.org/news/how-does-a-tiny-bird-like-a-winter-wren-manage-to-sing-so-many-sounds-so-quickly-and-so-loudly/) the sound power of a crowing rooster.

[^3]: Evolutionary biologists call this ["tail-flagging."](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12181754/) When a rabbit runs, the flashing white tail acts as a visual alarm to others nearby. Furthermore, it can confuse predators: a fox locking eyes on a flashing white target can get momentarily disoriented when the rabbit suddenly pivots and the white tail disappears into the dark grass, causing the predator to overshoot its pounce.

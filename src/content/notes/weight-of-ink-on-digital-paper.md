For some time now, this website lived in a *Digital Default*.

It used some standard off-white and black, with safe, neutral greys: the kind of hues that come pre-installed in design frameworks to avoid offending anyone. But as I spent more time writing it began to feel thin. The palette lacked gravity.

In this recent redesign, I wanted the site to feel like a physical document -- a high-quality cotton paper stock with a dense, organic ink.

I gave the background the warmer hue of <code style="background-color: #f9f8f5; border: 1px solid var(--color-border);">oatmeal paper</code>. This was matched in the primary typography with a deep <code style="background-color: #121211; color: #f9f8f5;">carbon ink</code>. A subtle shift, but it changed the optical weight of the words. On a screen, pure black can feel harsh and clinical. By adding a microscopic drop of warmth to the text and the background, the letters finally felt like they were sitting *in* the paper rather than floating above it. The weathered greys of <code style="background-color: #4a4947; color: #f9f8f5;">iron ore</code> and <code style="background-color: #706f6c; color: #f9f8f5;">warm stone</code> complemented these as secondary and tertiary textual alternatives.

And if the page is paper and the letters are ink, the accent tincture needed to be an *artisan's mark*. In this case, <code style="background-color: #264027; color: #f9f8f5;">English ivy</code>. It is a blackened, organic green that represents the rooted nature of this work. I've used it sparingly.

It is, hopefully, a digital space that will feel as permanent as a well-bound book.

<small>N.B.</small> -- Naming colours is an art form in itself; one I have yet to master.
